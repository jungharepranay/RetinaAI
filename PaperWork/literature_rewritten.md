Advances in general-purpose image recognition, particularly those catalysed by the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) and its corpus of over 1.2 million training images spanning 1,000 categories, laid the essential groundwork for deep learning in medical image analysis. He et al. [1] addressed a paradox that had stalled progress in network design: deeper architectures, contrary to expectation, yielded higher training error than shallower ones. Their solution, deep residual learning (ResNet), introduced skip (shortcut) connections that permit gradient flow to bypass one or more convolutional layers, thereby resolving this degradation problem. The proposed ResNet-152, a 152-layer convolutional network containing approximately 60 million parameters, achieved a top-5 error rate of 3.57% on the ILSVRC-2015 benchmark, surpassing human-level performance for the first time in the competition's history. The residual learning framework is formally expressed as H(x) = F(x) + x, wherein F(x) represents the learned residual mapping and x the identity shortcut; operationally, the network learns incremental corrective adjustments rather than complete transformations. What is less frequently acknowledged, however, is the degree to which this architecture's success on natural images translates to the markedly different domain of retinal fundus photography. The pretrained residual features do capture hierarchical visual patterns transferable to pathological structures in retinal images, yet the statistical properties of medical images (low inter-class variance, subtle lesion morphology, variable imaging conditions) diverge substantially from those of the ImageNet training distribution. A complementary perspective on feature extraction was offered by Szegedy et al. [2], who proposed the Inception architecture (GoogLeNet). This design introduced multi-scale feature extraction within a single network layer through parallel convolutional branches of varying kernel sizes (1x1, 3x3, and 5x5 filters alongside 3x3 max-pooling), concatenated to form a rich composite representation. The resulting "network within a network" philosophy reduced the parameter count to approximately 6.8 million (roughly 12 times fewer than AlexNet) while achieving a top-5 error rate of 6.67% on ILSVRC-2014. For retinal image analysis, this multi-scale capability is particularly relevant. Microaneurysms occupy only a few pixels, whereas optic disc changes and macular degeneration affect substantially larger retinal regions; a fixed-scale feature extractor would inevitably privilege one spatial granularity at the expense of another. Simonyan and Zisserman [3] pursued a different design philosophy with VGGNet, showing that architectural simplicity (uniform 3x3 convolutional filters across all layers) combined with increased depth (16 and 19 weight layers in VGG-16 and VGG-19 respectively) could achieve competitive performance, reaching a top-5 error rate of 7.3% on ILSVRC-2014. The 138 million parameters of VGGNet make it computationally expensive; the architecture's uniform structure and highly transferable intermediate features nevertheless established it as the most widely adopted backbone for transfer learning in early medical imaging research. A salient observation from this work concerns the finding that two stacked 3x3 convolutions provide the same effective receptive field as a single 5x5 filter while employing fewer parameters and introducing an additional non-linearity, an insight that has influenced all subsequent architecture designs. Building upon the principle of feature reuse rather than additive residual correction, Huang et al. [4] proposed DenseNet (Densely Connected Convolutional Networks). In this architecture, each layer receives direct input from all preceding layers within a dense block via channel-wise concatenation, as opposed to the element-wise addition employed in ResNet. DenseNet-121, comprising 121 layers organized into four dense blocks with growth rate k = 32, achieved 25.02% top-1 error on ImageNet while using only approximately 8 million parameters, substantially fewer than both ResNet-50 (25.5M) and VGG-16 (138M). This parameter efficiency arises because dense connections encourage feature reuse across layers, effectively reducing feature redundancy. For medical imaging tasks characterised by limited training data (typically fewer than 10,000 images in retinal disease datasets compared to millions in ImageNet), such efficiency is consequential: it mitigates overfitting while maintaining expressive capacity. DenseNet's dense connectivity also facilitates implicit deep supervision, as earlier layers receive gradient signals directly from the loss function through shortened gradient paths, enabling them to learn more discriminative low-level features such as retinal vessel edges and lesion boundaries. It is perhaps unsurprising, then, that DenseNet has become a preferred backbone in medical imaging settings where labelled data scarcity is the binding constraint. More recently, Tan and Le [5] introduced EfficientNet, which addresses the question of how to systematically scale CNN architectures for maximum accuracy per FLOP. Through a neural architecture search (NAS) process, they first designed a baseline network (EfficientNet-B0) and then proposed a compound scaling method that uniformly scales network width (number of channels), depth (number of layers), and input resolution using a compound coefficient phi, with scaling factors alpha^phi for depth, beta^phi for width, and gamma^phi for resolution, subject to the constraint alpha times beta-squared times gamma-squared approximately equal to 2. This principled scaling approach produced a family of models spanning EfficientNet-B0 (5.3M parameters, 77.1% top-1 accuracy) to EfficientNet-B7 (66M parameters, 84.3% top-1 accuracy), with each variant achieving superior accuracy-efficiency trade-offs compared to existing models. EfficientNet-B3, used in the proposed RetinAI ensemble system, comprises approximately 12 million parameters with an input resolution of 224x224, achieving 81.6% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet. Whether this efficiency advantage persists when the architecture is transferred to retinal images, where the distribution shift from ImageNet is considerable, is a question that the original authors did not address but which has direct bearing on clinical deployment decisions.

The direct application of these foundational architectures to retinal disease screening can be traced to the study of Gulshan et al. [6], who developed and validated a deep learning algorithm specifically for detecting diabetic retinopathy from retinal fundus photographs. The study employed an Inception-v3 architecture pretrained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on a large proprietary dataset of 128,175 retinal images, each graded 3 to 7 times by a panel of 54 US-licensed board-certified ophthalmologists to establish a high-quality reference standard. Validation was performed on two independent clinical datasets: EyePACS-1 (9,963 images from community clinics in the United States) and MESSIDOR-2 (1,748 images from three French ophthalmology departments), yielding an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.991 on EyePACS-1 and 0.990 on MESSIDOR-2 at the operating point chosen for high sensitivity. At the selected threshold, the system achieved 97.5% sensitivity and 93.4% specificity for detecting referable diabetic retinopathy (moderate non-proliferative DR or worse), performance that exceeded all seven individual ophthalmologists on the validation panel. These results established that deep learning could achieve ophthalmologist-level diagnostic accuracy for DR screening. The system was, however, limited to binary classification (referable versus non-referable DR), did not grade DR severity according to the ETDRS scale, could not detect co-existing conditions such as glaucoma or AMD, and relied on a proprietary training dataset that precludes independent reproduction. The reliance on proprietary data is not a trivial concern; it fundamentally limits external validation and renders the reported performance metrics difficult to contextualise against subsequent open-benchmark studies. Extending this work in scope and demographic breadth, Ting et al. [7] developed and validated a deep learning system using retinal images from 76,370 patients drawn from the Singapore Integrated Diabetic Retinopathy Programme (SiDRP) and 10 multiethnic cohorts comprising 494,661 retinal images. The system employed a modified VGGNet architecture and was distinctive in its multi-disease scope, simultaneously screening for referable DR, vision-threatening DR, possible glaucoma, and AMD using independent binary classification heads. The reported AUCs were 0.936 for referable DR, 0.958 for vision-threatening DR, 0.942 for possible glaucoma suspect (based on vertical CDR greater than or equal to 0.7 or neuroretinal rim narrowing), and 0.931 for AMD. The clinical significance of this study is threefold: it showed that a single deep learning system could screen for multiple retinal diseases simultaneously; it validated performance across highly diverse populations including Chinese, Malay, Indian, Caucasian, and African American cohorts; and it operated within an established national screening infrastructure, suggesting practical clinical deployability. A methodological tension, however, merits careful attention. The system treated each disease as an independent binary classification problem using separate softmax outputs, fundamentally precluding the modelling of disease co-occurrence patterns; for instance, the well-established clinical association between diabetic retinopathy and hypertensive retinopathy in patients with metabolic syndrome cannot be captured by architecturally independent classification heads. This structural limitation is precisely the problem that later multi-label approaches sought to address, though not without introducing trade-offs of their own. Gargeya and Leng [8] proposed an end-to-end data-driven deep learning approach for automated DR identification using a custom five-layer convolutional neural network architecture. The study used a training dataset of 75,137 colour fundus images and validated performance on two independent external datasets: MESSIDOR-2 (1,748 images) and eOphtha (463 images). The system achieved an AUC of 0.97 on the primary dataset with 94% sensitivity and 98% specificity, and AUCs of 0.94 and 0.95 on the external validation sets respectively, using five-fold cross-validation for primary evaluation. Of particular significance was the generation of automated abnormality heatmaps (data-driven attention maps highlighting regions that contributed most to the positive DR prediction). Whether such coarse visualisations constitute clinically actionable evidence, however, remains an open question. The heatmaps were unstructured, offering no attribution to specific clinical features such as exudates, haemorrhages, or microaneurysms, and their interpretive value for clinicians requiring structured diagnostic evidence is therefore limited. Abramoff et al. [9] took a different approach, integrating deep learning features with handcrafted clinical descriptors (including haemorrhage detectors, exudate detectors, and texture features) in a hybrid system for automated DR detection. The system was evaluated on the publicly available MESSIDOR-2 dataset (1,748 images from 874 diabetic patients), reporting an AUC of 0.980 with 96.8% sensitivity and 87.0% specificity at the reference standard operating point. The emphasis on evaluation using a publicly available, independently collected dataset was itself a contribution of considerable import, representing a critical step toward clinical deployment and reproducibility that many earlier studies had not taken. This work was also among the earliest to show that combining learned deep features with domain-specific handcrafted features could improve detection performance over either approach alone, presaging the hybrid methodology adopted by the proposed RetinAI system. The validation dataset was, however, relatively small (1,748 images), and the system was limited to binary DR detection without severity grading or multi-disease capability. The tension between classification accuracy and interpretability that runs through [6], [7], [8], and [9] is never explicitly acknowledged by these authors, yet it is precisely this gap that motivates the hybrid clinical reasoning pipelines discussed in subsequent sections.

For disease-specific detection beyond DR, the automated identification of glaucomatous optic neuropathy has received considerable attention, driven by the clinical need for early detection of a condition that causes irreversible vision loss. Li et al. [10] developed and validated a deep learning system specifically for detecting glaucomatous optic neuropathy (GON) based on colour fundus photographs. Using a ResNet-50 architecture with transfer learning from ImageNet, the study employed a retrospective dataset of 48,116 fundus images from the Chinese community population and the LabelMe online annotation repository, labelled by a panel of trained graders with adjudication by glaucoma specialists. The system achieved an AUC of 0.986 with 95.6% sensitivity and 92.0% specificity on its primary validation set. Additional validation on three external datasets (SCES with 1,542 images, SINDI with 4,095 images, and BES with 487 images) yielded AUCs of 0.977, 0.959, and 0.888 respectively, indicating strong but not uniform generalisability across different ethnic populations and imaging equipment. The decline to 0.888 on the BES dataset is instructive, as it suggests that domain shift arising from differences in camera systems and patient demographics may erode performance more than aggregate metrics from single-centre studies would imply. A critical limitation of this system was its reliance on image-level glaucoma labels without explicit estimation of the cup-to-disc ratio (CDR), the most widely used clinical biomarker for glaucoma, where a CDR exceeding 0.6 in Caucasian populations (or 0.7 in African populations) is considered suspicious. This disconnect between the model's classification mechanism and the clinician's diagnostic reasoning process exemplifies the broader challenge of clinical interpretability in deep learning systems. Orlando et al. [11] directly addressed the CDR estimation challenge through the REFUGE (Retinal Fundus Glaucoma Challenge), which established a unified benchmark framework for evaluating automated glaucoma assessment methods encompassing three subtasks: optic disc segmentation, optic cup segmentation, and binary glaucoma classification from fundus photographs. The challenge dataset comprised 1,200 images (400 training, 400 validation, 400 test) with pixel-level annotations for optic disc and optic cup boundaries. Twelve international teams submitted solutions employing diverse architectures including U-Net, DeepLabv3+, Attention-gated networks, and multi-task cascaded networks. The best-performing method for optic disc segmentation achieved a Dice coefficient of 0.960, while the best optic cup segmentation method achieved a Dice of 0.872. CDR estimation error was below 0.05 for the top methods, and the best glaucoma classification AUC reached 0.882. These results showed that deep segmentation-based CDR estimation can approach clinical-grade accuracy, providing a methodological foundation for incorporating spatial biomarkers into classification pipelines. The relatively small dataset (1,200 images) and the segmentation-only evaluation scope, without integration into a broader multi-disease classification framework, limited its direct applicability to comprehensive screening systems; this remains a gap that the field has yet to convincingly close. Burlina et al. [12] applied deep convolutional neural networks to the automated grading of age-related macular degeneration from colour fundus images. The study used the Age-Related Eye Disease Study (AREDS) dataset comprising over 130,000 fundus images from 4,613 participants, with AMD severity graded according to the AREDS simplified scale. Two architectures were evaluated: OverFeat (a precursor to modern CNNs based on multi-scale sliding window detection) and VGGNet with transfer learning from ImageNet. The binary classification task distinguished between "no or early AMD" (AREDS categories 1 to 2) and "referable intermediate/advanced AMD" (AREDS categories 3 to 4), with the VGGNet-based model achieving an accuracy of 88.4% and a Cohen's kappa of 0.764, indicating substantial agreement with the reference standard defined by certified graders at reading centres. This kappa value was comparable to inter-grader agreement among human experts, validating the clinical acceptability of the automated system. The binary formulation (referable versus non-referable), however, precluded fine-grained staging of AMD severity. More consequentially, the complete absence of any form of explainability (no heatmaps, no feature attribution, no clinical reasoning) raised concerns about clinical trust and regulatory approval. In a domain where treatment decisions depend on precise staging, an opaque classifier that only reports a binary outcome, however accurate, may prove insufficient for clinical adoption.

The evolution toward multi-label retinal disease classification, wherein a single fundus image may simultaneously exhibit multiple co-existing pathologies, represents a critical paradigm shift from the binary and multi-class approaches described above. Clinical reality compels this transition: the ODIR-5K dataset reveals extensive disease co-occurrence, with diabetes and other abnormalities co-appearing in 497 of 7,439 samples, and hypertension co-occurring with diabetes in 89 samples. Wang et al. [13] proposed an EfficientNet-based multi-label classification system specifically designed for the ODIR-5K benchmark, which contains 7,439 retinal fundus images annotated across eight diagnostic categories: Normal (N), Diabetes (D), Glaucoma (G), Cataract (C), Age-related Macular Degeneration (A), Hypertension (H), Myopia (M), and Other Abnormalities (O). Their approach employed an ensemble of EfficientNet variants (B0 through B4) with sigmoid activation functions at the output layer for independent per-class probability estimation, replacing the softmax activation used in multi-class formulations. Binary cross-entropy loss was computed independently for each of the eight output neurons. The system incorporated test-time augmentation (horizontal flipping, multi-scale cropping) and model ensembling via probability averaging, achieving 98.7% accuracy, 97.7% precision, 98.7% recall, and 98.2% F1-score on the ODIR competition test set. These aggregate metrics warrant careful contextualisation given the class distribution. The ODIR evaluation protocol and test/validation split configurations vary across studies; the dominant Normal and Diabetes classes (representing over 56% of all labels) inflate aggregate accuracy metrics; and the system provided no form of clinical explainability or feature validation. The absence of per-class metrics (for example, per-class F1 for rare conditions like Hypertension with only 203 samples) precludes meaningful assessment of performance on underrepresented pathological categories. It would be premature to conclude, on the basis of these aggregate figures alone, that the system is clinically viable for detecting rare conditions such as hypertensive retinopathy. Li et al. [14] introduced CANet (Cross-disease Attention Network), a novel architecture for joint diabetic retinopathy and diabetic macular edema grading that explicitly models inter-disease correlations. The architecture incorporates two attention modules: a Disease-Specific Attention Module (DSAM), which learns separate spatial attention maps for DR and DME to highlight disease-relevant regions, and a Disease-Dependent Attention Module (DDAM), which captures the mutual influence between DR and DME grades through cross-disease feature interaction. Built on a ResNet-50 backbone and evaluated on the IDRiD dataset (516 images with per-pixel annotations and image-level DR and DME grades) and MESSIDOR (1,200 images with DR grades), CANet achieved quadratic weighted kappa values of 0.829 for DR grading and 0.886 for DME grading, representing statistically significant improvements over the baseline ResNet-50 without attention (kappa = 0.785 for DR and kappa = 0.845 for DME). The key contribution was showing that explicitly modelling the clinical relationship between two co-occurring diseases (DR and DME frequently co-exist because DME is often a complication of DR) improves grading accuracy compared to independent classification heads that ignore this correlation. Whether this attention-based correlation modelling extends to eight or more diverse conditions, some of which have no clinical correlation (e.g., cataract and myopia), remains an open research challenge that the available evidence does not resolve.

The black-box nature of deep learning models presents a persistent barrier to their clinical adoption in high-stakes medical decision-making, and this challenge has generated a substantial body of work on visual and post-hoc explanation techniques. Selvaraju et al. [15] proposed Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM), a technique that generates coarse localisation maps highlighting important regions in the input image for a specific CNN prediction. Unlike its predecessor CAM (Class Activation Mapping), which required the network to have a specific architecture with a global average pooling layer immediately before the output, Grad-CAM is applicable to any CNN architecture by computing the gradient of the target class score with respect to the feature maps of any convolutional layer, followed by global average pooling of these gradients to obtain importance weights per channel. The resulting heatmap is computed as a ReLU-activated weighted combination of forward activation maps, producing a coarse localisation map at the spatial resolution of the chosen convolutional layer. Evaluated on VGG-16 and ResNet architectures across ImageNet classification and PASCAL VOC localisation tasks, Grad-CAM improved class-discriminative localisation accuracy by 10.4% over CAM and showed that it could effectively distinguish between fine-grained categories based on the spatial distribution of attention. For medical imaging, Grad-CAM has become the de facto standard for CNN interpretability, enabling clinicians to verify whether model attention aligns with pathologically relevant regions such as exudates, haemorrhages, or the optic disc. The resolution of Grad-CAM visualisations, however, is inherently limited by the spatial dimensions of the final convolutional layer's feature maps (typically 7x7 or 14x14 for 224x224 input). The heatmaps provide no structured clinical information: they indicate where the model is attending but not what clinical feature it has identified, a distinction that is consequential for clinical workflows where actionable diagnostic reasoning, not merely spatial attention, is required. Rajpurkar et al. [16] applied DenseNet-121 to multi-label chest radiograph interpretation in a system named CheXNet, directly confirming the transferability of both the DenseNet architecture and the Grad-CAM explainability framework to medical multi-label classification. CheXNet was trained on the ChestX-ray14 dataset comprising 112,120 frontal-view chest X-ray images annotated with 14 pathology labels (including pneumonia, cardiomegaly, effusion, and nodule) using natural language processing of associated radiology reports. The DenseNet-121 backbone was pretrained on ImageNet, with the final classification layer replaced by a 14-neuron dense layer with sigmoid activation for multi-label prediction. The system achieved a mean AUC of 0.7632 across all 14 pathologies, with individual AUCs ranging from 0.506 for hernia to 0.895 for cardiomegaly. For pneumonia specifically, CheXNet achieved an AUC of 0.7680, exceeding the average radiologist performance (AUC 0.633 computed from four practising radiologists). This study established two critical precedents for the proposed RetinAI system: first, that DenseNet-121 (one of the core architectures in the proposed ensemble) with sigmoid outputs and binary cross-entropy loss is an effective and parameter-efficient architecture for multi-label medical image classification; and second, that Grad-CAM heatmaps overlaid on medical images provide clinically meaningful visual explanations that aid in diagnostic reasoning, even when classification performance is imperfect. The wide range of per-class AUCs (0.506 to 0.895) is itself instructive, as it reveals how aggregate metrics can obscure severe performance deficits on individual conditions, a pattern that recurs across multi-label medical imaging studies and has direct bearing on the evaluation of the ODIR-5K systems discussed earlier. Tjoa and Guan [17] provided a comprehensive survey on explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) in the context of medical applications, published in IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks and Learning Systems. The survey systematically categorised over 200 XAI methods into seven taxonomic families: perturbation-based methods (e.g., LIME, SHAP) that assess feature importance by systematically occluding or modifying input regions; backpropagation-based methods (e.g., Grad-CAM, Guided Backpropagation, DeconvNet) that use gradient signals to attribute predictions to input features; attention mechanism-based methods that embed interpretability into the model architecture itself through learned attention weights; concept-based methods that map internal representations to human-understandable concepts; example-based methods that explain predictions by reference to similar training instances; rule-based methods that distil complex model behaviour into human-readable decision rules; and intrinsically interpretable models such as decision trees and generalised additive models. The review argued that clinical trust in AI requires not merely accurate predictions but transparent reasoning, and identified three key requirements for medical XAI: fidelity (the explanation must accurately reflect the model's decision process), comprehensibility (the explanation must be understandable to clinicians without machine learning expertise), and sufficiency (the explanation must provide enough information for the clinician to accept or override the prediction). This taxonomy directly informs the multi-modal explainability strategy of the proposed RetinAI system, which combines backpropagation-based visual explanations (Grad-CAM) with rule-based clinical validation. Sengupta et al. [18] conducted a critical review of deep learning techniques applied to fundus images for ophthalmic diagnosis, published in Artificial Intelligence in Medicine. The review analysed over 100 published studies spanning DR detection, glaucoma screening, AMD classification, and retinal vessel segmentation. A central argument of the review was that while end-to-end deep learning systems achieve impressive aggregate classification metrics, they fail to provide the structured clinical features that ophthalmologists rely upon for diagnosis: the cup-to-disc ratio for glaucoma, the presence and count of microaneurysms for early DR detection, the presence of drusen for AMD staging, and the degree of retinal vessel attenuation for hypertensive retinopathy. Clinically meaningful intermediate representations, not merely final classification scores, are essential for building physician trust and enabling clinical integration. This perspective has direct bearing on the design of the proposed RetinAI system, whose hybrid architecture explicitly computes classical clinical biomarkers (exudate count, haemorrhage count, microaneurysm count, CDR, vessel visibility, texture variance, edge density) alongside CNN predictions and cross-validates them through literature-backed rules.

Ronneberger et al. [19] proposed U-Net for biomedical image segmentation, introducing an encoder-decoder architecture with symmetric skip connections that concatenate feature maps from the contracting path to the corresponding expansive path, thereby combining high-resolution spatial information from early layers with semantic context from deeper layers. U-Net was originally designed for neuronal structure segmentation in electron microscopy images (ISBI 2012 challenge dataset comprising only 30 training images with dimensions of 512x512 pixels), achieving an IoU (Intersection over Union) of 0.920 and winning the challenge by a significant margin. The architecture's most consequential contribution was its ability to achieve high-quality segmentation performance with extremely limited training data through extensive data augmentation, including elastic deformations that simulate realistic biological tissue variations. In the domain of retinal image analysis, U-Net and its variants (Attention U-Net, U-Net++, and nnU-Net) have been widely adopted for three critical segmentation tasks: retinal blood vessel extraction from fundus images, optic disc and optic cup delineation for CDR computation, and lesion segmentation for DR grading. The segmentation-based approach to CDR estimation, as validated in the REFUGE Challenge [11], represents a clinically superior alternative to the brightness-thresholding approach currently employed in the proposed RetinAI system. A persistent methodological challenge that pervades retinal disease datasets is class imbalance, where the label distribution is severely skewed toward common conditions. Lin et al. [20] addressed this challenge through the focal loss, a reformulation of the standard cross-entropy that has had broad impact beyond its original object detection context. In the ODIR-5K dataset, for example, Diabetes and Normal together constitute over 56% of all labels, while Hypertension represents only 2.7%, creating a 10:1 imbalance ratio between the most and least frequent classes. Standard binary cross-entropy loss treats all samples equally, causing the optimiser to be dominated by the loss contributions from the abundant easy-to-classify normal samples while under-learning the rare but clinically important pathological patterns. Focal loss addresses this by introducing a modulating factor (1 minus p_t) raised to the power gamma to the standard cross-entropy, where p_t is the model's estimated probability for the correct class and gamma (greater than or equal to zero) is a tunable focusing parameter. When gamma is greater than zero, the loss for well-classified examples (p_t approaching 1) is down-weighted exponentially, directing the training gradient toward hard, misclassified examples that typically belong to rare pathological categories. With gamma set to 2 (the recommended setting) and an additional class-balancing factor alpha equal to 0.25, focal loss achieved 39.1% AP on the COCO benchmark for dense object detection using the proposed RetinaNet architecture, a 3.2% improvement over standard cross-entropy. In the context of multi-label retinal disease classification, focal loss has been widely adopted to mitigate the detrimental effect of class imbalance on the detection of rare conditions such as hypertension, AMD, and other abnormalities; the proposed RetinAI system handles class imbalance through a different mechanism, dynamically computing class-specific positive weights for a weighted standard binary cross-entropy loss. Whether this weighting strategy is superior to, inferior to, or complementary with focal loss for the specific class distribution of ODIR-5K is an empirical question that the current study addresses through ablation.

Despite these substantial advances spanning foundational architectures, clinical validation studies, multi-label classification techniques, explainability methods, and class imbalance solutions, several critical research gaps persist that collectively motivate the proposed RetinAI system. The overwhelming majority of retinal AI systems, including the high-profile studies by Gulshan et al. [6], Ting et al. [7], and Li et al. [10], operate as standalone CNN classifiers that produce disease probability scores without cross-referencing these predictions against classical clinical biomarkers such as exudate counts, haemorrhage patterns, or the cup-to-disc ratio. This practice departs fundamentally from the diagnostic reasoning process employed by ophthalmologists, who routinely integrate visual pattern recognition with quantitative clinical measurements and patient history. The disconnect is not merely academic; it has practical implications for clinical acceptance, as physicians are unlikely to trust a system whose reasoning they cannot interrogate against their own clinical knowledge. Multi-label detection on comprehensive datasets such as ODIR-5K remains a challenging open problem, with most systems achieving macro F1-scores below 0.50 due to severe class imbalance (10:1 ratio between dominant and rare classes) and the heterogeneous, often subtle visual presentation of rare conditions like hypertensive retinopathy and early AMD. The high aggregate accuracy metrics reported by studies such as Wang et al. [13] (98.7%) are dominated by performance on the majority classes and may obscure poor performance on clinically critical minority conditions, a point that the preceding analysis of per-class evaluation gaps has reinforced. Explainability in existing systems is typically limited to post-hoc gradient-based heatmaps (Grad-CAM), which indicate where the model attends but provide no structured clinical reasoning, no literature-backed validation, and no integration with established clinical guidelines such as the ETDRS for DR severity grading or the AAO Preferred Practice Patterns for glaucoma management. No existing system in the reviewed literature incorporates an interactive patient questionnaire to refine AI predictions based on symptom history and risk factors, a practice that is fundamental to clinical ophthalmology, where patient-reported symptoms (blurred vision, metamorphopsia, floaters, eye pressure sensation) and medical history (diabetes duration, blood pressure status, family history of glaucoma, refractive error) significantly influence diagnostic confidence. The proposed RetinAI system addresses all four of these gaps by integrating ensemble-based multi-label classification (EfficientNet-B3, DenseNet-121, ConvNeXt-Tiny) with classical computer vision feature extraction (exudates, haemorrhages, microaneurysms, CDR, texture variance, vessel visibility, edge density), a literature-backed rule-based clinical validation engine citing ETDRS Report No. 10, AAO Preferred Practice Patterns, Jonas ISNT Rule, Keith-Wagener-Barker Classification, and META-PM Study Group guidelines, Grad-CAM visual explainability with interactive comparison sliders, and a two-phase interactive diagnostic workflow that generates disease-specific clinical questions and contextualises findings based on patient-reported answers (while strictly preserving the raw CNN confidence scores), constituting a comprehensive hybrid clinical pipeline that more faithfully mirrors the multi-modal, multi-step diagnostic process of expert ophthalmologists than any existing system in the reviewed literature.
