# I. Introduction

Retinal diseases such as diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma, age-related macular degeneration, and hypertensive retinopathy rank among the leading causes of preventable blindness globally. The clinical reality is that timely screening depends on skilled ophthalmologists, and there simply are not enough of them to meet the demand, particularly in resource-limited settings. Automated retinal image analysis powered by deep learning has advanced significantly over the past decade, with several systems approaching or exceeding expert-level sensitivity on individual disease detection tasks. These systems, however, share a common structural limitation that becomes apparent when one considers how ophthalmologists actually make diagnostic decisions.

In clinical practice, a retinal specialist does not simply look at a fundus image and output a disease label. The process involves recognising specific lesion patterns (microaneurysms, exudates, haemorrhages), measuring quantitative biomarkers (cup-to-disc ratio, vessel calibre), cross-referencing findings against established grading scales (ETDRS for diabetic retinopathy, AREDS for macular degeneration), and integrating patient history including systemic conditions and reported symptoms. Most published deep learning systems collapse this multi-step reasoning process into a single forward pass through a neural network, producing a probability score and perhaps a Grad-CAM heatmap, but offering no structured clinical evidence, no biomarker cross-validation, and no mechanism for incorporating patient context. The result is a system that may achieve impressive aggregate metrics on benchmark datasets but provides little of the interpretive scaffolding that clinicians need to trust and act on its outputs.

This paper presents RetinAI, a hybrid framework designed to address this gap. The classification backbone is an ensemble of three architecturally distinct convolutional neural networks: EfficientNet-B3, DenseNet-121, and ConvNeXt-Tiny. These three architectures were selected specifically because they embody different inductive biases (compound scaling, dense feature reuse, and modernised convolutional design respectively), and their predictions are combined through per-class AUC-weighted soft voting rather than uniform averaging so that the ensemble can adapt to the substantial variation in classification difficulty across the eight ODIR-5K disease categories. Running in parallel with the deep learning branch, a classical computer vision module extracts 17 clinically relevant biomarkers directly from the raw fundus image, including lesion counts, vascular morphology features, texture descriptors, and the cup-to-disc ratio. A rule-based validation engine then cross-references these extracted features against the ensemble predictions, generating structured supporting or contradicting flags grounded in established guidelines such as ETDRS, AAO Preferred Practice Patterns, the Jonas ISNT Rule, and the Keith-Wagener-Barker classification.

Two additional components complete the pipeline. Grad-CAM heatmaps generated from the EfficientNet-B3 backbone provide spatial visualisation of which regions of the fundus image influenced each prediction, giving clinicians a visual anchor for evaluating model outputs. An interactive patient questionnaire captures demographic data, systemic conditions, and self-reported vision complaints, which are used to contextualise the diagnostic findings and generate personalised risk assessments. Crucially, all post-inference modules operate as read-only consumers of the ensemble outputs: patient context and validation flags enrich the clinical report but never alter the underlying predicted probabilities, preserving calibration and ensuring full auditability. The entire system is deployed as a web-based platform using FastAPI, generating a structured ten-section PDF screening report within a single request cycle. This combination of ensemble classification, biomarker extraction, literature-backed validation, gradient-based explainability, and patient context integration is, to the best of our knowledge, not present in any existing system in the reviewed literature.
