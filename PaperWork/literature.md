The modern era of deep learning for medical image analysis was fundamentally enabled by breakthroughs in general-purpose image recognition architectures developed on the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC), which comprises over 1.2 million training images across 1,000 categories. He et al. [1] introduced deep residual learning (ResNet) with the key innovation of skip (shortcut) connections that allow gradient flow to bypass one or more convolutional layers, thereby resolving the degradation problem wherein deeper networks paradoxically produce higher training error than their shallower counterparts. The proposed ResNet-152, consisting of 152 convolutional layers with approximately 60 million parameters, achieved a top-5 error rate of 3.57% on the ILSVRC-2015 benchmark — surpassing human-level performance for the first time in the competition's history. The residual learning framework is mathematically formulated as H(x) = F(x) + x, where F(x) represents the learned residual mapping and x the identity shortcut, enabling the network to learn incremental refinements rather than complete transformations. This architecture has become a foundational backbone for transfer learning in medical imaging, as the pretrained residual features naturally capture hierarchical visual patterns transferable to pathological structures in retinal fundus images. Szegedy et al. [2] proposed the Inception architecture (GoogLeNet), which introduced the concept of multi-scale feature extraction within a single network layer through parallel convolutional branches of varying kernel sizes (1×1, 3×3, and 5×5 filters alongside 3×3 max-pooling), concatenated to form a rich feature representation. This "network within a network" design philosophy reduced the parameter count to approximately 6.8 million — roughly 12 times fewer than AlexNet — while achieving a top-5 error rate of 6.67% on ILSVRC-2014. The multi-scale approach is particularly advantageous for retinal image analysis, where pathological features span vastly different spatial scales: microaneurysms occupy only a few pixels, while optic disc changes and macular degeneration affect larger retinal regions. Simonyan and Zisserman [3] demonstrated through VGGNet that architectural simplicity with uniform 3×3 convolutional filters across all layers, combined with increased network depth (16 and 19 weight layers in VGG-16 and VGG-19 respectively), could achieve state-of-the-art performance with a top-5 error rate of 7.3% on ILSVRC-2014. VGGNet's 138 million parameters make it computationally expensive; however, its uniform architecture and highly transferable intermediate features established it as the most widely adopted backbone for transfer learning in early medical imaging applications. The key insight that two stacked 3×3 convolutions provide the same effective receptive field as a single 5×5 filter while employing fewer parameters and introducing an additional non-linearity has influenced all subsequent architecture designs. Building upon the principle of feature reuse, Huang et al. [4] proposed DenseNet (Densely Connected Convolutional Networks), wherein each layer receives direct input from all preceding layers within a dense block via channel-wise concatenation, rather than through element-wise addition as in ResNet. DenseNet-121, comprising 121 layers organized into four dense blocks with growth rate k = 32, achieved 25.02% top-1 error on ImageNet while using only approximately 8 million parameters — substantially fewer than both ResNet-50 (25.5M) and VGG-16 (138M). This parameter efficiency arises because dense connections encourage feature reuse across layers, effectively reducing feature redundancy. For medical imaging tasks characterized by limited training data — typically fewer than 10,000 images in retinal disease datasets compared to millions in ImageNet — this parameter-efficient architecture is particularly advantageous, as it mitigates overfitting while maintaining expressive capacity. DenseNet's dense connectivity also facilitates implicit deep supervision, as earlier layers receive gradient signals directly from the loss function through shortened paths, enabling them to learn more discriminative low-level features such as retinal vessel edges and lesion boundaries. More recently, Tan and Le [5] introduced EfficientNet, which addresses the question of how to systematically scale CNN architectures for maximum accuracy per FLOP. Through a neural architecture search (NAS) process, they first designed a baseline network (EfficientNet-B0) and then proposed a compound scaling method that uniformly scales network width (number of channels), depth (number of layers), and input resolution using a compound coefficient φ, with scaling factors α^φ for depth, β^φ for width, and γ^φ for resolution, subject to the constraint α · β² · γ² ≈ 2. This principled scaling approach produced a family of models from EfficientNet-B0 (5.3M parameters, 77.1% top-1 accuracy) to EfficientNet-B7 (66M parameters, 84.3% top-1 accuracy), with each variant achieving superior accuracy-efficiency trade-offs compared to existing models. EfficientNet-B3, used in the proposed RetinAI ensemble system, comprises approximately 12 million parameters with an input resolution of 224×224, achieving 81.6% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet — making it a strong candidate for retinal image classification where computational resources and dataset sizes are limited.
The direct application of these foundational architectures to retinal disease screening was pioneered by the landmark study of Gulshan et al. [6], who developed and validated a deep learning algorithm specifically for the detection of diabetic retinopathy from retinal fundus photographs. The study employed an Inception-v3 architecture pretrained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on a large proprietary dataset of 128,175 retinal images, each graded 3–7 times by a panel of 54 US-licensed board-certified ophthalmologists to establish a high-quality reference standard. The algorithm was validated on two independent clinical datasets: EyePACS-1 (9,963 images from community clinics in the United States) and MESSIDOR-2 (1,748 images from three French ophthalmology departments), achieving an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.991 on EyePACS-1 and 0.990 on MESSIDOR-2 at the operating point chosen for high sensitivity. At the selected threshold, the system achieved 97.5% sensitivity and 93.4% specificity for detecting referable diabetic retinopathy (moderate non-proliferative DR or worse), performance that exceeded all seven individual ophthalmologists on the validation panel. This study was transformative in establishing that deep learning could achieve ophthalmologist-level diagnostic accuracy for DR screening; however, the system was limited to binary classification (referable vs. non-referable DR), did not grade DR severity according to the ETDRS scale, could not detect co-existing conditions such as glaucoma or AMD, and relied on a proprietary training dataset that limits reproducibility. Building on this breakthrough, Ting et al. [7] developed and validated a deep learning system using retinal images from 76,370 patients drawn from the Singapore Integrated Diabetic Retinopathy Programme (SiDRP) and 10 multiethnic cohorts comprising 494,661 retinal images. The system employed a modified VGGNet architecture and was unique in its multi-disease scope, simultaneously screening for referable DR, vision-threatening DR, possible glaucoma, and AMD using independent binary classification heads. The reported AUCs were 0.936 for referable DR, 0.958 for vision-threatening DR, 0.942 for possible glaucoma suspect (based on vertical CDR ≥ 0.7 or neuroretinal rim narrowing), and 0.931 for AMD. The study was clinically significant for three reasons: first, it demonstrated that a single deep learning system could screen for multiple retinal diseases simultaneously; second, it validated performance across highly diverse populations including Chinese, Malay, Indian, Caucasian, and African American cohorts; and third, it operated within an established national screening infrastructure, suggesting practical clinical deployability. Nevertheless, the system treated each disease as an independent binary classification problem using separate softmax outputs, fundamentally precluding the modeling of disease co-occurrence patterns — for example, the well-established clinical association between diabetic retinopathy and hypertensive retinopathy in patients with metabolic syndrome. Gargeya and Leng [8] proposed an end-to-end data-driven deep learning approach for automated DR identification using a custom five-layer convolutional neural network architecture. The study utilized a training dataset of 75,137 color fundus images and validated performance on two independent external datasets: MESSIDOR-2 (1,748 images) and eOphtha (463 images). The system achieved an AUC of 0.97 on the primary dataset with 94% sensitivity and 98% specificity, and AUCs of 0.94 and 0.95 on the external validation sets respectively, using five-fold cross-validation for primary evaluation. A key contribution of this work was the generation of automated abnormality heatmaps — data-driven attention maps highlighting regions that contributed most to the positive DR prediction. However, these heatmaps were coarse, unstructured visualizations without attribution to specific clinical features (exudates, hemorrhages, or microaneurysms), limiting their interpretive value for clinicians requiring structured diagnostic evidence. Abràmoff et al. [9] demonstrated improved automated DR detection through a hybrid approach that integrated deep learning features with handcrafted clinical descriptors, including hemorrhage detectors, exudate detectors, and texture features. The system was evaluated on the publicly available MESSIDOR-2 dataset (1,748 images from 874 diabetic patients), reporting an AUC of 0.980 with 96.8% sensitivity and 87.0% specificity at the reference standard operating point. The notable contribution of this study was its emphasis on evaluation using a publicly available, independently collected dataset — a critical step toward clinical deployment and reproducibility that many earlier studies lacked. Furthermore, this work was among the earliest to demonstrate that combining learned deep features with domain-specific handcrafted features could improve detection performance over either approach alone, presaging the hybrid methodology adopted by the proposed RetinAI system. Nonetheless, the validation dataset was relatively small (1,748 images), and the system was limited to binary DR detection without severity grading or multi-disease capability.
For disease-specific detection beyond DR, Li et al. [10] developed and validated a deep learning system specifically for detecting glaucomatous optic neuropathy (GON) based on color fundus photographs. Using a ResNet-50 architecture with transfer learning from ImageNet, the study employed a retrospective dataset of 48,116 fundus images from the Chinese community population and the LabelMe online annotation repository, labeled by a panel of trained graders with adjudication by glaucoma specialists. The system achieved an AUC of 0.986 with 95.6% sensitivity and 92.0% specificity on its primary validation set. The model was additionally validated on three external datasets — SCES (1,542 images), SINDI (4,095 images), and BES (487 images) — achieving AUCs of 0.977, 0.959, and 0.888 respectively, demonstrating strong but not uniform generalizability across different ethnic populations and imaging equipment. A critical limitation was the reliance on image-level glaucoma labels without explicit estimation of the cup-to-disc ratio (CDR), the most widely used clinical biomarker for glaucoma where a CDR exceeding 0.6 in Caucasian populations (or 0.7 in African populations) is considered suspicious. This disconnect between the model's classification mechanism and the clinician's diagnostic reasoning process exemplifies the broader challenge of clinical interpretability in deep learning systems. Orlando et al. [11] directly addressed the CDR estimation challenge through the REFUGE (Retinal Fundus Glaucoma Challenge), which established a unified benchmark framework for evaluating automated glaucoma assessment methods encompassing three subtasks: optic disc segmentation, optic cup segmentation, and binary glaucoma classification from fundus photographs. The challenge dataset comprised 1,200 images (400 training, 400 validation, 400 test) with pixel-level annotations for optic disc and optic cup boundaries. Twelve international teams submitted solutions employing diverse architectures including U-Net, DeepLabv3+, Attention-gated networks, and multi-task cascaded networks. The best-performing method for optic disc segmentation achieved a Dice coefficient of 0.960, while the best optic cup segmentation method achieved a Dice of 0.872. CDR estimation error was below 0.05 for the top methods, and the best glaucoma classification AUC reached 0.882. The REFUGE results demonstrated that deep segmentation-based CDR estimation can approach clinical-grade accuracy, providing a methodological foundation for incorporating spatial biomarkers into classification pipelines. However, the relatively small dataset (1,200 images) and the segmentation-only evaluation scope — without integration into a broader multi-disease classification framework — limited its direct applicability to comprehensive screening systems. Burlina et al. [12] applied deep convolutional neural networks to the automated grading of age-related macular degeneration from color fundus images. The study leveraged the Age-Related Eye Disease Study (AREDS) dataset comprising over 130,000 fundus images from 4,613 participants, with AMD severity graded according to the AREDS simplified scale. Two architectures were evaluated: OverFeat (a precursor to modern CNNs based on multi-scale sliding window detection) and VGGNet with transfer learning from ImageNet. The binary classification task distinguished between "no or early AMD" (AREDS categories 1–2) and "referable intermediate/advanced AMD" (AREDS categories 3–4), with the VGGNet-based model achieving an accuracy of 88.4% and a Cohen's kappa (κ) of 0.764, indicating substantial agreement with the reference standard defined by certified graders at reading centers. This κ value was comparable to inter-grader agreement among human experts, validating the clinical acceptability of the automated system. However, the binary formulation (referable vs. non-referable) precluded fine-grained staging of AMD severity, and the absence of any form of explainability — no heatmaps, no feature attribution, no clinical reasoning — raised concerns about clinical trust and regulatory approval.
The evolution toward multi-label retinal disease classification — wherein a single fundus image may simultaneously exhibit multiple co-existing pathologies — represents a critical paradigm shift from the binary and multi-class approaches described above. This transition is motivated by clinical reality: the ODIR-5K dataset reveals extensive disease co-occurrence, with diabetes and other abnormalities co-appearing in 497 of 7,439 samples, and hypertension co-occurring with diabetes in 89 samples. Wang et al. [13] proposed an EfficientNet-based multi-label classification system specifically designed for the ODIR-5K benchmark, which contains 7,439 retinal fundus images annotated across eight diagnostic categories — Normal (N), Diabetes (D), Glaucoma (G), Cataract (C), Age-related Macular Degeneration (A), Hypertension (H), Myopia (M), and Other Abnormalities (O). Their approach employed an ensemble of EfficientNet variants (B0 through B4) with sigmoid activation functions at the output layer for independent per-class probability estimation, replacing the softmax activation used in multi-class formulations. Binary cross-entropy loss was computed independently for each of the eight output neurons. The system incorporated test-time augmentation (horizontal flipping, multi-scale cropping) and model ensembling via probability averaging, achieving 98.7% accuracy, 97.7% precision, 98.7% recall, and 98.2% F1-score on the ODIR competition test set. While these metrics are impressive, they should be interpreted with significant caution: the ODIR evaluation protocol and test/validation split configurations vary across studies, the dominant Normal and Diabetes classes (representing over 56% of all labels) inflate aggregate accuracy metrics, and the system provided no form of clinical explainability or feature validation. The absence of per-class metrics (e.g., per-class F1 for rare conditions like Hypertension with only 203 samples) makes it difficult to assess the system's viability for detecting underrepresented pathologies. Li et al. [14] introduced CANet (Cross-disease Attention Network), a novel architecture for joint diabetic retinopathy and diabetic macular edema grading that explicitly models inter-disease correlations. The architecture incorporates two novel attention modules: a Disease-Specific Attention Module (DSAM), which learns separate spatial attention maps for DR and DME to highlight disease-relevant regions, and a Disease-Dependent Attention Module (DDAM), which captures the mutual influence between DR and DME grades through cross-disease feature interaction. The network was built on a ResNet-50 backbone and evaluated on the IDRiD dataset (516 images with per-pixel annotations and image-level DR and DME grades) and MESSIDOR (1,200 images with DR grades). On IDRiD, CANet achieved quadratic weighted kappa values of 0.829 for DR grading and 0.886 for DME grading, representing statistically significant improvements over the baseline ResNet-50 without attention (κ = 0.785 for DR and κ = 0.845 for DME). The key contribution was demonstrating that explicitly modeling the clinical relationship between two co-occurring diseases — DR and DME frequently co-exist because DME is often a complication of DR — improves grading accuracy compared to independent classification heads that ignore this correlation. However, CANet's scope was limited to only two related conditions with an inherent clinical dependency; extending this attention-based correlation modeling to eight or more diverse conditions (some of which have no clinical correlation, e.g., cataract and myopia) remains an open research challenge.
The black-box nature of deep learning models presents a significant barrier to their clinical adoption in high-stakes medical decision-making. Selvaraju et al. [15] proposed Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM), a technique that generates coarse localization maps highlighting important regions in the input image for a specific CNN prediction. Unlike its predecessor CAM (Class Activation Mapping), which required the network to have a specific architecture with a global average pooling layer immediately before the output, Grad-CAM is applicable to any CNN architecture by computing the gradient of the target class score with respect to the feature maps of any convolutional layer, followed by global average pooling of these gradients to obtain importance weights per channel. The resulting heatmap is computed as a ReLU-activated weighted combination of forward activation maps, producing a coarse localization map at the spatial resolution of the chosen convolutional layer. Evaluated on VGG-16 and ResNet architectures across ImageNet classification and PASCAL VOC localization tasks, Grad-CAM improved class-discriminative localization accuracy by 10.4% over CAM and demonstrated that it could effectively distinguish between fine-grained categories based on the spatial distribution of attention. For medical imaging, Grad-CAM has become the de facto standard for CNN interpretability, enabling clinicians to verify whether model attention aligns with pathologically relevant regions such as exudates, hemorrhages, or the optic disc. However, the resolution of Grad-CAM visualizations is inherently limited by the spatial dimensions of the final convolutional layer's feature maps (typically 7×7 or 14×14 for 224×224 input), and the heatmaps provide no structured clinical information — they show "where" the model is looking but not "what" clinical feature it has identified. Rajpurkar et al. [16] applied DenseNet-121 to multi-label chest radiograph interpretation in a system named CheXNet, directly demonstrating the transferability of both the DenseNet architecture and the Grad-CAM explainability framework to medical multi-label classification. CheXNet was trained on the ChestX-ray14 dataset comprising 112,120 frontal-view chest X-ray images annotated with 14 pathology labels (including pneumonia, cardiomegaly, effusion, and nodule) using natural language processing of associated radiology reports. The DenseNet-121 backbone was pretrained on ImageNet, with the final classification layer replaced by a 14-neuron dense layer with sigmoid activation for multi-label prediction. The system achieved a mean AUC of 0.7632 across all 14 pathologies, with individual AUCs ranging from 0.506 for hernia to 0.895 for cardiomegaly. For pneumonia specifically, CheXNet achieved an AUC of 0.7680, exceeding the average radiologist performance (AUC 0.633 computed from four practicing radiologists). This study established two critical precedents for the proposed RetinAI system: first, that DenseNet-121 (one of the core architectures in our ensemble) with sigmoid outputs and binary cross-entropy loss is an effective and parameter-efficient architecture for multi-label medical image classification; and second, that Grad-CAM heatmaps overlaid on medical images provide clinically meaningful visual explanations that aid in diagnostic reasoning, even when classification performance is imperfect. Tjoa and Guan [17] provided a comprehensive survey on explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) in the context of medical applications, published in IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks and Learning Systems. The survey systematically categorized over 200 XAI methods into seven taxonomic families: (1) perturbation-based methods (e.g., LIME, SHAP) that assess feature importance by systematically occluding or modifying input regions; (2) backpropagation-based methods (e.g., Grad-CAM, Guided Backpropagation, DeconvNet) that use gradient signals to attribute predictions to input features; (3) attention mechanism-based methods that embed interpretability into the model architecture itself through learned attention weights; (4) concept-based methods that map internal representations to human-understandable concepts; (5) example-based methods that explain predictions by reference to similar training instances; (6) rule-based methods that distill complex model behavior into human-readable decision rules; and (7) intrinsically interpretable models such as decision trees and generalized additive models. The review argued that clinical trust in AI requires not merely accurate predictions but transparent reasoning, and identified three key requirements for medical XAI: fidelity (the explanation must accurately reflect the model's decision process), comprehensibility (the explanation must be understandable to clinicians without machine learning expertise), and sufficiency (the explanation must provide enough information for the clinician to accept or override the prediction). This taxonomy directly informs the multi-modal explainability strategy of the proposed RetinAI system, which combines backpropagation-based visual explanations (Grad-CAM) with rule-based clinical validation. Sengupta et al. [18] conducted a critical review of deep learning techniques applied to fundus images for ophthalmic diagnosis, published in Artificial Intelligence in Medicine. The review analyzed over 100 published studies spanning DR detection, glaucoma screening, AMD classification, and retinal vessel segmentation. A central argument of the review was that while end-to-end deep learning systems achieve impressive aggregate classification metrics, they fail to provide the structured clinical features that ophthalmologists rely upon for diagnosis: the cup-to-disc ratio for glaucoma, the presence and count of microaneurysms for early DR detection, the presence of drusen for AMD staging, and the degree of retinal vessel attenuation for hypertensive retinopathy. The authors emphasized that clinically meaningful intermediate representations — not merely final classification scores — are essential for building physician trust and enabling clinical integration. This perspective directly motivates the proposed RetinAI system's hybrid architecture, which explicitly computes classical clinical biomarkers (exudate count, hemorrhage count, microaneurysm count, CDR, vessel visibility, texture variance, edge density) alongside CNN predictions and cross-validates them through literature-backed rules.
Ronneberger et al. [19] proposed U-Net for biomedical image segmentation, introducing an encoder-decoder architecture with symmetric skip connections that concatenate feature maps from the contracting path to the corresponding expansive path, thereby combining high-resolution spatial information from early layers with semantic context from deeper layers. U-Net was originally designed for neuronal structure segmentation in electron microscopy images (ISBI 2012 challenge dataset comprising only 30 training images with dimensions of 512×512 pixels), achieving an IoU (Intersection over Union) of 0.920 — winning the challenge by a significant margin. The architecture's most impactful innovation was its ability to achieve state-of-the-art segmentation performance with extremely limited training data through extensive data augmentation, including elastic deformations that simulate realistic biological tissue variations. In the domain of retinal image analysis, U-Net and its variants (Attention U-Net, U-Net++, and nnU-Net) have been widely adopted for three critical segmentation tasks: retinal blood vessel extraction from fundus images, optic disc and optic cup delineation for CDR computation, and lesion segmentation for DR grading. The segmentation-based approach to CDR estimation, as validated in the REFUGE Challenge [11], represents a clinically superior alternative to the brightness-thresholding approach currently employed in the proposed RetinAI system. An important methodological contribution addressing class imbalance — a pervasive and critical challenge in retinal disease datasets where the label distribution is severely skewed — is the focal loss proposed by Lin et al. [20]. In the ODIR-5K dataset, for example, Diabetes and Normal together constitute over 56% of all labels, while Hypertension represents only 2.7%, creating a 10:1 imbalance ratio between the most and least frequent classes. Standard binary cross-entropy loss treats all samples equally, causing the optimizer to be dominated by the loss contributions from the abundant easy-to-classify normal samples while under-learning the rare but clinically important pathological patterns. Focal loss addresses this by introducing a modulating factor (1−p_t)^γ to the standard cross-entropy, where p_t is the model's estimated probability for the correct class and γ ≥ 0 is a tunable focusing parameter. When γ > 0, the loss for well-classified examples (p_t → 1) is down-weighted exponentially, directing the training gradient toward hard, misclassified examples that typically belong to rare pathological categories. With γ = 2 (the recommended setting) and an additional class-balancing factor α = 0.25, focal loss achieved 39.1% AP on the COCO benchmark for dense object detection using the proposed RetinaNet architecture — a 3.2% improvement over standard cross-entropy. In the context of multi-label retinal disease classification, focal loss has been widely adopted to mitigate the detrimental effect of class imbalance on the detection of rare conditions such as hypertension, AMD, and other abnormalities; the proposed RetinAI system handles class imbalance by dynamically computing class-specific positive weights for a weighted standard binary cross-entropy loss.
Despite these substantial advancements spanning foundational architectures, clinical validation studies, multi-label classification techniques, explainability methods, and class imbalance solutions, several critical research gaps persist that collectively motivate the proposed RetinAI system. First, the overwhelming majority of retinal AI systems — including the high-profile studies by Gulshan et al. [6], Ting et al. [7], and Li et al. [10] — operate as standalone CNN classifiers that produce disease probability scores without cross-referencing these predictions against classical clinical biomarkers such as exudate counts, hemorrhage patterns, or the cup-to-disc ratio. This practice departs fundamentally from the diagnostic reasoning process employed by ophthalmologists, who routinely integrate visual pattern recognition with quantitative clinical measurements and patient history. Second, multi-label detection on comprehensive datasets such as ODIR-5K remains a challenging open problem, with most systems achieving macro F1-scores below 0.50 due to severe class imbalance (10:1 ratio between dominant and rare classes) and the heterogeneous, often subtle visual presentation of rare conditions like hypertensive retinopathy and early AMD. The high aggregate accuracy metrics reported by studies such as Wang et al. [13] (98.7%) are dominated by performance on the majority classes and may obscure poor performance on clinically critical minority conditions. Third, explainability in existing systems is typically limited to post-hoc gradient-based heatmaps (Grad-CAM), which show where the model attends but provide no structured clinical reasoning, no literature-backed validation, and no integration with established clinical guidelines such as the ETDRS for DR severity grading or the AAO Preferred Practice Patterns for glaucoma management. Fourth, no existing system in the reviewed literature incorporates an interactive patient questionnaire to refine AI predictions based on symptom history and risk factors — a practice that is fundamental to clinical ophthalmology, where patient-reported symptoms (blurred vision, metamorphopsia, floaters, eye pressure sensation) and medical history (diabetes duration, blood pressure status, family history of glaucoma, refractive error) significantly influence diagnostic confidence. The proposed RetinAI system addresses all four of these gaps by integrating an ensemble-based multi-label classification (EfficientNet-B3, DenseNet-121, ConvNeXt-Tiny) with classical computer vision feature extraction (exudates, hemorrhages, microaneurysms, CDR, texture variance, vessel visibility, edge density), a literature-backed rule-based clinical validation engine citing ETDRS Report No. 10, AAO Preferred Practice Patterns, Jonas ISNT Rule, Keith-Wagener-Barker Classification, and META-PM Study Group guidelines, Grad-CAM visual explainability with interactive comparison sliders, and a two-phase interactive diagnostic workflow that generates disease-specific clinical questions and contextualizes findings based on patient-reported answers (while strictly preserving the raw CNN confidence scores) — constituting a comprehensive hybrid clinical pipeline that more faithfully mirrors the multi-modal, multi-step diagnostic process of expert ophthalmologists than any existing system in the literature survey
