The deep learning architectures that now underpin most retinal image analysis systems were not originally designed for medical applications at all. They emerged from the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC), a benchmark of over 1.2 million natural images spanning 1,000 categories, and their subsequent transfer to clinical domains reflects both the power and the limitations of large-scale pretraining. He et al. [1] tackled what was then a puzzling observation: adding more layers to a convolutional network did not always improve performance and sometimes made it worse. Their deep residual learning framework (ResNet) resolved this degradation problem by introducing skip connections that allow gradient flow to bypass convolutional layers, effectively letting the network learn residual corrections rather than complete input-to-output mappings. Formally, the residual block computes H(x) = F(x) + x, where F(x) is the learned residual and x passes through unchanged. ResNet-152 (152 layers, approximately 60 million parameters) achieved a top-5 error rate of 3.57% on ILSVRC-2015, beating human-level accuracy for the first time. The architecture quickly became the default backbone for transfer learning in medical imaging, though it is worth questioning how well features learned from photographs of dogs and aeroplanes transfer to the subtle lesion patterns found in retinal fundus images. The statistical properties of these two domains differ considerably, and the effectiveness of the transfer depends heavily on the fine-tuning protocol. Szegedy et al. [2] took a different approach with the Inception architecture (GoogLeNet), which processes the input through parallel convolutional branches of varying kernel sizes (1x1, 3x3, 5x5, and 3x3 max-pooling) within a single layer, concatenating the outputs to capture features at multiple spatial scales simultaneously. This design reduced the parameter count to roughly 6.8 million (about 12 times fewer than AlexNet) while reaching a top-5 error rate of 6.67% on ILSVRC-2014. The multi-scale property is directly relevant to retinal imaging because pathological features vary enormously in size: microaneurysms may occupy only a handful of pixels, while optic disc abnormalities and areas of macular degeneration span much larger regions. A fixed-scale architecture inevitably compromises on one end of this spectrum. VGGNet, proposed by Simonyan and Zisserman [3], showed that a simpler strategy could also work well. By stacking uniform 3x3 convolutional filters to a depth of 16 or 19 layers, VGG-16 and VGG-19 achieved a top-5 error rate of 7.3% on ILSVRC-2014. The model is large (138 million parameters) and computationally expensive, but its straightforward architecture and the strong transferability of its intermediate features made it the most popular choice for early medical imaging transfer learning. The practical insight that two stacked 3x3 convolutions have the same receptive field as a single 5x5 filter but use fewer parameters and add an extra nonlinearity has shaped every major architecture since.

Huang et al. [4] pushed the idea of feature reuse further with DenseNet, where each layer in a dense block receives the concatenated feature maps of every preceding layer rather than just adding a residual as in ResNet. DenseNet-121 (121 layers, four dense blocks, growth rate k = 32) reached 25.02% top-1 error on ImageNet with only about 8 million parameters, far fewer than ResNet-50 (25.5M) or VGG-16 (138M). The parameter efficiency comes from the fact that dense connections encourage feature reuse across layers, reducing redundancy. For retinal disease datasets, which typically contain fewer than 10,000 labelled images, this efficiency matters a great deal: fewer parameters mean less risk of overfitting when training data is scarce. Dense connectivity also creates short paths from early layers to the loss function, providing implicit deep supervision that helps the network learn discriminative low-level features such as vessel edges and lesion boundaries. The architecture has since become a frequently chosen backbone for medical imaging tasks where labelled data scarcity is the primary constraint. Tan and Le [5] addressed a question that the preceding architectures left largely unanswered: given a fixed computational budget, how should one allocate resources between network depth, width, and input resolution? Their compound scaling method, applied through EfficientNet, uses a neural architecture search to find a baseline network (EfficientNet-B0) and then scales all three dimensions simultaneously using a compound coefficient, subject to the constraint that the total computation roughly doubles with each step. The resulting family ranges from EfficientNet-B0 (5.3M parameters, 77.1% top-1 accuracy) to EfficientNet-B7 (66M parameters, 84.3% top-1 accuracy). EfficientNet-B3, which is used in the proposed RetinAI ensemble, has approximately 12 million parameters at an input resolution of 224x224 and achieves 81.6% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet. Whether these efficiency gains fully survive the domain shift to retinal images is an empirical question that the original paper did not address.

The transition from general-purpose architectures to retinal disease screening began in earnest with Gulshan et al. [6], who trained an Inception-v3 network on 128,175 retinal fundus images to detect diabetic retinopathy. The training labels came from a panel of 54 US-licensed ophthalmologists, each image graded between 3 and 7 times, producing an unusually high-quality reference standard. On two independent validation sets, EyePACS-1 (9,963 images) and MESSIDOR-2 (1,748 images), the system achieved AUCs of 0.991 and 0.990 respectively, with 97.5% sensitivity and 93.4% specificity for detecting referable diabetic retinopathy at the chosen operating point. These numbers exceeded all seven individual ophthalmologists on the validation panel. The study was widely regarded as proof that deep learning could match expert-level performance for DR screening, but several limitations deserve closer attention. The system performed only binary classification (referable vs. non-referable DR), with no severity grading, no detection of co-existing conditions like glaucoma or AMD, and no explainability. Perhaps more consequentially, the training dataset was proprietary, which means other groups cannot independently verify or reproduce the reported performance. This matters because the field has repeatedly seen performance figures that do not replicate when models are tested on data from different populations or imaging equipment. Ting et al. [7] addressed the question of scope, building a system that could screen for four conditions simultaneously: referable DR, vision-threatening DR, possible glaucoma, and AMD. The training data was far more diverse than most previous studies, drawing from 76,370 patients in the Singapore Integrated Diabetic Retinopathy Programme and 10 multiethnic cohorts totalling 494,661 images. Using a modified VGGNet, they reported AUCs of 0.936 for referable DR, 0.958 for vision-threatening DR, 0.942 for possible glaucoma (CDR greater than or equal to 0.7 or neuroretinal rim narrowing), and 0.931 for AMD. The clinical value was threefold: multi-disease screening from a single system, validation across ethnically diverse populations, and deployment within an existing national screening programme. There is, however, a structural issue with the architecture. Each disease was handled by an independent binary classification head with a separate softmax output, meaning the model had no mechanism for capturing disease co-occurrence patterns. Diabetic retinopathy and hypertensive retinopathy frequently co-occur in patients with metabolic syndrome, but this architecture treats them as unrelated prediction tasks.

Gargeya and Leng [8] used a custom five-layer CNN trained on 75,137 fundus images, achieving an AUC of 0.97 for DR detection with 94% sensitivity and 98% specificity, with external validation AUCs of 0.94 on MESSIDOR-2 and 0.95 on eOphtha using five-fold cross-validation. Their contribution was the generation of automated abnormality heatmaps, data-driven attention maps showing which regions influenced the positive DR prediction. The problem is that these heatmaps are coarse and unstructured. They show roughly where the model is paying attention but provide no information about what clinical feature (exudate, haemorrhage, microaneurysm) has been identified. For a clinician deciding whether to refer a patient, a red blob over the macula is considerably less useful than a structured finding such as "three microaneurysms detected in the temporal arcade." Abramoff et al. [9] took a hybrid approach that has direct relevance to the RetinAI design philosophy. Their system combined deep learning features with handcrafted clinical descriptors (haemorrhage detectors, exudate detectors, texture features) and was evaluated on the publicly available MESSIDOR-2 dataset (1,748 images), achieving an AUC of 0.980 with 96.8% sensitivity and 87.0% specificity. The decision to evaluate on a public dataset, while seemingly unremarkable, was actually an important step at a time when most high-profile studies used proprietary data. This work was also among the first to show that combining learned features with domain-specific engineered features outperformed either approach in isolation, an observation that directly influenced the dual-branch architecture of the proposed RetinAI system. The validation set was small, though, and the system was restricted to binary DR detection with no severity grading or multi-disease capability. Across all four of these clinical studies [6]-[9], a common pattern is visible: strong aggregate performance metrics paired with limited or absent explainability and no mechanism for cross-referencing predictions against established clinical biomarkers.

Glaucoma detection presents a different set of challenges. Li et al. [10] trained a ResNet-50 on 48,116 fundus images to detect glaucomatous optic neuropathy, with labels assigned by trained graders and adjudicated by glaucoma specialists. The primary validation AUC was 0.986 with 95.6% sensitivity and 92.0% specificity. External validation on three datasets (SCES with 1,542 images, SINDI with 4,095 images, BES with 487 images) yielded AUCs of 0.977, 0.959, and 0.888. The drop to 0.888 on BES is telling: it suggests that differences in camera systems and patient demographics can substantially erode performance in ways that single-centre validation would not reveal. A more fundamental issue concerns what the model actually learns. The system was trained on image-level glaucoma labels without explicit cup-to-disc ratio estimation, yet CDR is the biomarker that ophthalmologists rely on most heavily (CDR exceeding 0.6 in Caucasian populations, or 0.7 in African populations, is considered suspicious). The model may have learned to detect glaucoma through entirely different image features than those a clinician would examine, making it difficult to assess when and why the model might fail. Orlando et al. [11] tackled CDR estimation directly through the REFUGE Challenge, which set up a standardised benchmark with 1,200 images (400 each for training, validation, and test) annotated with pixel-level optic disc and cup boundaries. Twelve international teams submitted solutions using architectures including U-Net, DeepLabv3+, and attention-gated networks. The best optic disc segmentation achieved a Dice coefficient of 0.960, the best optic cup segmentation reached 0.872, CDR estimation error was below 0.05 for top methods, and the best glaucoma classification AUC was 0.882. These results showed that segmentation-based CDR estimation can approach clinical-grade accuracy. The dataset was small, however, and the evaluation scope was limited to segmentation without integration into a broader classification framework. Burlina et al. [12] applied deep learning to AMD grading using the AREDS dataset (over 130,000 fundus images from 4,613 participants), comparing OverFeat and VGGNet for binary classification between "no or early AMD" (AREDS categories 1-2) and "referable intermediate/advanced AMD" (AREDS categories 3-4). The VGGNet model achieved 88.4% accuracy and a Cohen's kappa of 0.764, comparable to inter-grader agreement among human experts. The binary formulation precludes fine-grained AMD staging, and the total absence of any explainability (no heatmaps, no feature attribution, nothing) raises questions about the path to clinical adoption. An accurate but opaque binary classifier may satisfy a research benchmark, but it provides little basis for the treatment decisions that depend on knowing the specific disease stage.

The shift toward multi-label classification, where a single fundus image can be labelled with multiple co-existing conditions, marks an important conceptual advance over the binary and multi-class approaches described above. This is not merely a technical refinement; it reflects clinical reality. In the ODIR-5K dataset, diabetes and other abnormalities co-occur in 497 of 7,439 samples, and hypertension co-occurs with diabetes in 89 samples. Wang et al. [13] built an EfficientNet-based multi-label system for ODIR-5K, using an ensemble of EfficientNet variants (B0 through B4) with sigmoid activations for independent per-class probability estimation and binary cross-entropy loss for each of the eight output neurons. Test-time augmentation and probability averaging across models produced 98.7% accuracy, 97.7% precision, 98.7% recall, and 98.2% F1-score on the ODIR competition test set. These numbers look excellent at first glance but require careful interpretation. The ODIR evaluation protocol and split configurations vary across studies, Normal and Diabetes together account for over 56% of all labels (which inflates aggregate metrics), and no per-class breakdown was provided. Without knowing the F1-score for Hypertension (approximately 203 samples in the entire dataset), one cannot assess whether the system can actually detect rare conditions at clinically useful rates. The system also provided no explainability of any kind. Li et al. [14] introduced CANet, which explicitly models the clinical relationship between diabetic retinopathy and diabetic macular edema through two attention modules: a Disease-Specific Attention Module that learns separate spatial attention maps for each condition, and a Disease-Dependent Attention Module that captures mutual influence between DR and DME grades. Built on ResNet-50 and evaluated on IDRiD (516 images) and MESSIDOR (1,200 images), CANet achieved quadratic weighted kappa values of 0.829 for DR grading and 0.886 for DME grading, statistically significant improvements over the baseline without attention (kappa = 0.785 for DR, kappa = 0.845 for DME). The insight that modelling disease correlation improves grading accuracy is valuable, since DR and DME frequently co-exist (DME is often a complication of DR). Extending this correlation modelling to eight diverse conditions, some of which have no clinical relationship to each other (cataract and myopia, for instance), is a different problem entirely, and one for which the available evidence offers no clear solution.

Explainability has become a central concern as deep learning systems move closer to clinical deployment. Selvaraju et al. [15] proposed Grad-CAM, which generates coarse localisation maps for any CNN without requiring architectural modifications (unlike its predecessor CAM, which needed a global average pooling layer before the output). Grad-CAM computes the gradient of the target class score with respect to the feature maps of any convolutional layer, applies global average pooling to obtain per-channel importance weights, and produces a heatmap as a ReLU-activated weighted combination of forward activation maps. On VGG-16 and ResNet applied to ImageNet and PASCAL VOC, Grad-CAM improved class-discriminative localisation by 10.4% over CAM. For medical imaging, it has become the standard interpretability tool. Its limitations, however, are real. The spatial resolution is set by the feature map dimensions (typically 7x7 or 14x14 for 224x224 input), and the heatmaps convey location ("where the model looked") but not identity ("what it found there"). These are quite different things clinically. Rajpurkar et al. [16] applied DenseNet-121 with sigmoid outputs to multi-label chest radiograph interpretation (CheXNet), training on ChestX-ray14 (112,120 images, 14 pathology labels). The mean AUC was 0.7632 across all pathologies, with individual values ranging from 0.506 for hernia to 0.895 for cardiomegaly. For pneumonia specifically, CheXNet achieved an AUC of 0.7680, exceeding the average radiologist performance of 0.633. This study established two things that matter for the RetinAI design: DenseNet-121 with sigmoid outputs and binary cross-entropy works well for multi-label medical classification, and Grad-CAM heatmaps on medical images provide clinically useful (if imperfect) visual feedback. The wide range of per-class AUCs (0.506 to 0.895) also illustrates a recurring problem: aggregate metrics can hide catastrophic failures on individual conditions.

Tjoa and Guan [17] surveyed over 200 XAI methods for medical applications, organising them into seven families: perturbation-based (LIME, SHAP), backpropagation-based (Grad-CAM, Guided Backpropagation), attention-based, concept-based, example-based, rule-based, and intrinsically interpretable models. Their central argument was that clinical trust requires more than accuracy; it requires transparent reasoning. They identified three criteria for medical XAI: fidelity (the explanation must reflect the actual decision process), comprehensibility (clinicians without ML expertise must understand it), and sufficiency (enough information for the clinician to accept or override the prediction). This taxonomy informed the multi-modal explainability design of RetinAI, which combines backpropagation-based visual explanations (Grad-CAM) with rule-based clinical validation. Sengupta et al. [18] reviewed over 100 studies on deep learning for fundus image analysis and made an argument that aligns closely with the motivation behind the RetinAI system: that end-to-end classification, however accurate in aggregate, fails to provide the structured clinical features that ophthalmologists actually use for diagnosis. The cup-to-disc ratio for glaucoma, microaneurysm counts for early DR, drusen for AMD staging, vessel attenuation for hypertensive retinopathy; these intermediate clinical features are what build physician trust and enable clinical integration. The proposed RetinAI system is designed around this exact principle, explicitly computing classical biomarkers (exudate count, haemorrhage count, microaneurysm count, CDR, vessel visibility, texture variance, edge density) alongside CNN predictions and cross-validating them through literature-backed rules.

Ronneberger et al. [19] proposed U-Net for biomedical image segmentation, an encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections that concatenate feature maps from the contracting path to the expansive path, combining spatial detail from early layers with semantic information from deeper ones. U-Net was originally developed for neuronal structure segmentation (the ISBI 2012 challenge, with only 30 training images at 512x512 resolution), achieving an IoU of 0.920 and winning the competition by a clear margin. Its ability to produce high-quality segmentations from very limited training data, using aggressive augmentation including elastic deformations, is what made it so influential. In retinal imaging, U-Net variants (Attention U-Net, U-Net++, nnU-Net) are now widely used for vessel segmentation, optic disc and cup delineation, and lesion segmentation. The segmentation-based CDR estimation validated in the REFUGE Challenge [11] represents a clinically sounder approach than the brightness-thresholding method currently used in the RetinAI system, and this is an acknowledged limitation of the current implementation. Class imbalance is a pervasive issue in retinal disease datasets, and Lin et al. [20] proposed focal loss to address it in the context of dense object detection. In ODIR-5K, Normal and Diabetes comprise over 56% of labels while Hypertension makes up only 2.7%, a 10:1 ratio between the most and least common classes. Standard binary cross-entropy treats all samples equally, so the optimiser is dominated by gradients from the abundant, easy-to-classify normal cases. Focal loss adds a modulating factor (1 minus p_t) raised to the power gamma, which down-weights well-classified examples and focuses training on hard cases. With gamma set to 2 and a class-balancing factor alpha of 0.25, focal loss achieved 39.1% AP on COCO using the RetinaNet architecture, a 3.2% gain over standard cross-entropy. For multi-label retinal classification, focal loss has been widely adopted to improve detection of rare conditions. The RetinAI system takes a different approach, dynamically computing class-specific positive weights for a weighted binary cross-entropy loss, and whether this strategy is better or worse than focal loss for the ODIR-5K distribution is an empirical question that the current study addresses through ablation.

Taken together, the literature reveals four persistent gaps that collectively motivated the design of the RetinAI system. First, the great majority of retinal AI systems operate as standalone classifiers that produce probability scores without checking them against classical clinical biomarkers. An ophthalmologist who suspects diabetic retinopathy will look for specific lesions (haemorrhages, exudates, microaneurysms); a model that produces a DR probability of 0.85 without referencing any of these features is asking the clinician to accept its judgment without supporting evidence. Second, multi-label detection on datasets like ODIR-5K remains genuinely difficult, with most systems achieving macro F1-scores below 0.50 once the inflating effect of dominant classes is accounted for. The 98.7% accuracy reported by Wang et al. [13] tells us mostly about performance on Normal and Diabetes cases, not about clinically critical rare conditions. Third, explainability stops at Grad-CAM heatmaps. These are useful but limited: they show where the model attended but not what clinical feature it identified, and they are not linked to any established clinical grading system. Fourth, no reviewed system incorporates patient-reported symptoms and medical history into the diagnostic workflow, despite the fact that clinicians consider factors like diabetes duration, blood pressure status, family history of glaucoma, and reported visual symptoms (blurred vision, floaters, metamorphopsia, pressure sensation) when forming diagnostic opinions. The proposed RetinAI system addresses all four gaps by integrating ensemble-based multi-label classification (EfficientNet-B3, DenseNet-121, ConvNeXt-Tiny) with classical biomarker extraction (exudates, haemorrhages, microaneurysms, CDR, texture variance, vessel visibility, edge density), a rule-based validation engine citing ETDRS Report No. 10, AAO Preferred Practice Patterns, Jonas ISNT Rule, Keith-Wagener-Barker Classification, and META-PM Study Group guidelines, Grad-CAM visual explainability with interactive comparison, and a two-phase diagnostic workflow that generates disease-specific clinical questions and contextualises findings based on patient-reported answers while strictly preserving the raw CNN confidence scores.
