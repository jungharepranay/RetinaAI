# I. Introduction

Retinal diseases such as diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma, age-related macular degeneration, and hypertension-induced retinopathy are among the leading causes of preventable blindness worldwide, necessitating early and accurate diagnosis through retinal fundus imaging. The growing patient burden and limited availability of expert ophthalmologists have created an urgent need for automated, reliable, and clinically interpretable diagnostic systems capable of screening for multiple conditions simultaneously. Existing deep learning approaches to retinal disease detection, while achieving high classification accuracy on benchmark datasets, suffer from several persistent limitations: they typically operate as single-model classifiers without cross-referencing predictions against classical clinical biomarkers, they report aggregate performance metrics that obscure poor sensitivity on rare conditions, their explainability is limited to coarse spatial heatmaps that convey where the model attends but not what clinical feature it has identified, and they neglect the integration of patient-reported symptom data that is fundamental to the ophthalmological diagnostic workflow.

This paper presents RetinAI, a hybrid artificial intelligence-based framework for multi-label retinal disease detection that integrates deep learning with classical clinical reasoning to address these gaps. The classification backbone consists of an ensemble of three architecturally diverse convolutional neural networks (EfficientNet-B3, DenseNet-121, and ConvNeXt-Tiny), whose per-class sigmoid outputs are fused through a per-class AUC-weighted soft-voting mechanism. This ensemble is designed to exploit the distinct inductive biases of compound-scaled, densely connected, and modernised convolutional architectures, reducing correlated prediction errors and improving generalisation across the eight diagnostic categories of the ODIR-5K dataset: Normal, Diabetes, Glaucoma, Cataract, Age-related Macular Degeneration, Hypertension, Myopia, and Other Abnormalities. To enhance clinical relevance and interpretability, the system incorporates a parallel classical computer vision branch that extracts 17 disease-specific biomarkers (including exudate count, haemorrhage count, microaneurysm count, cup-to-disc ratio, vessel tortuosity, and drusen count) directly from the raw fundus image, and these features are cross-referenced against ensemble predictions through a rule-based validation engine grounded in established ophthalmological guidelines such as ETDRS, AAO Preferred Practice Patterns, the Jonas ISNT Rule, and the Keith-Wagener-Barker classification.

Grad-CAM-based visual explanations are generated from the EfficientNet-B3 backbone to highlight regions influencing model predictions, improving transparency and clinical trust. A distinctive aspect of the proposed system is its two-phase interactive diagnostic workflow, where initial predictions are augmented using a structured patient questionnaire that captures demographic information, systemic conditions, and self-reported vision concerns. These inputs are used to contextualise findings and generate personalised risk assessments without modifying the underlying model confidence scores, a design constraint that preserves statistical calibration while enabling clinically meaningful interpretation. The complete pipeline is deployed as a web-based clinical decision support platform using FastAPI, producing a structured ten-section PDF screening report with embedded Grad-CAM visualisations within a single request cycle. This combination of ensemble-based classification, classical biomarker extraction, literature-backed clinical validation, gradient-based explainability, and patient context integration positions RetinAI as a comprehensive decision-support tool for real-world retinal disease screening.
